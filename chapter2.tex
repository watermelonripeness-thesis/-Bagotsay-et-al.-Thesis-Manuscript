\section{Theoretical Framework}
    This study is grounded in Statistical Learning Theory (SLT), which provides a framework for understanding how a system can learn from data and make predictions on new, unseen samples. SLT explains the principles behind learning patterns from representative data, helping guide the design of models that can classify watermelon ripeness reliably (Du et al., 2025).
SLT is particularly relevant in this context because it provides a theoretical basis for using measurable features extracted from sensory data, specifically the acoustic signals produced by standardized mechanical knocks and the images of the watermelon’s ground spot. These features are processed using Digital Signal Processing (DSP) and Computer Vision (CV) methods, respectively, transforming raw data into quantifiable inputs suitable for classification.
Following the SLT framework, the system extracts features from acoustic signals and color-based features from images. These features feed into a classification model designed to assess ripeness accurately and consistently. By integrating these methods, the proposed device offers a portable, non-destructive, and theoretically sound approach to determining watermelon ripeness, providing farmers with an objective alternative to traditional, experience-based methods.

\subsection{Digital Signal Processing}

Manual determination of fruit and vegetable ripeness often involves simple acoustic or tactile cues — for example, farmers tapping a watermelon and listening for differences in sound or feeling firmness changes. Such practices implicitly rely on the idea that internal physical differences manifest as audible or vibrational signal changes (Hamid, N. A., et al., 2023). Digital Signal Processing formalizes this intuition by capturing acoustic signals (e.g., recorded tapping sounds) and transforming them using techniques such as the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) to extract features like dominant frequency and spectral amplitude, which correlate with internal structural properties. In Okafor’s (2020) study, a prototype watermelon ripeness tester used a microphone to record thumping sounds and then converted them into FFT waveforms using MATLAB, showing that frequency magnitude patterns could distinguish ripe from unripe watermelons.
Further supporting this, recent research in watermelon maturity classification demonstrates that frequency‑domain analysis of acoustic signals collected from different parts of the watermelon significantly enhances ripeness discrimination when combined with machine learning models such as SVM and logistic regression, improving classification reliability for future mobile‑based ripeness detection systems (Li, Y., Bu, Y., Guo, W., 2025). DSP is also central to other non‑destructive quality detection technologies, where preprocessing, filtering, and feature extraction improve the signal‑to‑noise ratio and enable robust characterization of physical fruit properties (Liu et al., 2025). These theoretical and empirical foundations illustrate that DSP is a key enabler for transforming raw sensor data into discriminative, actionable features for automated ripeness analysis.

\subsection{Machine Learning}
Machine learning is crucial in predicting fruit ripeness due to its ability to learn complex patterns from high‑dimensional data and to generalize across variations in environment, sensor conditions, and biological variability. Reviews of AI‑driven fruit maturity and quality detection confirm that ML models — including deep learning and traditional classifiers — significantly outperform manual and heuristic approaches by extracting meaningful relationships between input features and quality labels (Jie, M. et al., 2024; Hamid, N. A., et al., 2025). These models can integrate diverse feature sets derived from acoustic DSP and image analysis to improve prediction accuracy and robustness.

For example, a comparative analysis of machine learning approaches applied to watermelon maturity using acoustic and quality features showed that Support Vector Machine (SVM) and LightGBM models achieved high classification accuracies (exceeding 97\%) when using frequency and amplitude acoustic features combined with quality indicators (Parvathy Nayana, N., et. al., 2025). This demonstrates that ML models can effectively interpret complex feature patterns and improve predictive performance in real‑world agricultural systems. Additionally, non‑destructive classification of fruit sweetness and quality using a fusion of acoustic signals and image processing techniques reached high performance with various ML classifiers, showing that combining modalities can enhance the reliability of ripeness prediction (Chawgien, K., Kiattisin, S., 2021).
Beyond classification, ML supports multimodal feature fusion and real‑time prediction, making it indispensable for portable detection systems that aggregate heterogeneous input data. Reviewing current technological trends in fruit maturity assessment shows a growing emphasis on ML‑powered models that can optimize feature selection, adjust to sensory noise, and deliver scalable performance across diverse crops and environments (Jie, M. et al., 2024).

\subsection{Computer Vision}
Computer vision is widely used in crop monitoring and fruit ripeness evaluation by extracting external visual indicators, such as color gradients, texture changes, shape features, and surface patterns, that correspond with maturity stages. Deep learning–based vision models, particularly convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and YOLO variants, have demonstrated high effectiveness in detecting and classifying fruit at various ripeness levels in both controlled and real field conditions. For example, intelligent detection of muskmelon ripeness in greenhouse environments using a YOLO‑RFEW model achieved strong detection accuracy while remaining lightweight and computationally efficient (Defang, X., et. al., 2024).

State‑of‑the‑art reviews note that computer vision techniques based on digital image processing and machine learning classifiers are widely used to extract color, texture, and shape features during fruit growth stages, enabling automated maturity estimation that circumvents the limitations of manual inspection (Jie, M. et al., 2024). Furthermore, airborne and UAV mounted deep learning vision systems have been applied to strawberry maturity detection with high classification performance, optimizing harvest timing and crop management. (Singh, R., Gadade, A. M., & Hussain, I., 2025).

These advances highlight the effectiveness of CV in precision agriculture, where imaging technology combined with AI models can detect subtle variations in external features that indicate different ripeness stages. Integrating CV with DSP‑derived internal features and ML classification frameworks creates a holistic, multimodal sensing system for ripeness detection, improving accuracy, reliability, and on‑farm applicability for portable tools like your proposed watermelon ripeness detector.

\section{Literature Review}

The following studies are related to the non-destructive assessment of watermelon ripeness using digital signal processing, machine learning, and computer vision. These works provide relevant methods and findings that support the development of the proposed system.

\subsection{Digital Signal Processing}

\subsubsection{Watermelon Sorting Process by Frequency Identification and Artificial Neural Network
}

The study investigates the long-standing challenge of accurately sorting watermelons based on ripeness—a process traditionally performed through manual tapping and subjective auditory judgment (Wongkalasin et al., 2023). The purpose of the research is to develop a more objective, consistent, and modernized method of quality assessment using a frequency-based approach combined with artificial neural networks. To achieve this, the researchers generated controlled sound signals that were transmitted through watermelon samples, collected the resulting vibration responses, and analyzed the signal frequency and amplitude using Fast Fourier Transform (FFT). These processed acoustic features were then used as inputs to a feed-forward neural network trained to differentiate between various ripeness levels. A total of 30 watermelons were tested, each cut open afterward for ground-truth validation of their actual internal quality.

The findings showed that 129 Hz and 172 Hz produced the most distinct amplitude patterns for differentiating unripe, ripe, and near-rotten fruits, allowing the neural network to classify watermelons with reasonable accuracy. The study concluded that frequency-based analysis integrated with neural network processing provides a modern, non-destructive approach to automated sorting. However, the researchers acknowledged that the system’s reliability is limited by the small sample size and the use of only one watermelon variety, meaning broader testing is required to fully validate accuracy and optimize performance across different cultivars. 

This study provides a key technical precedent for the proposed acoustic analysis. It validates that processed sound signals (using FFT) can classify ripeness via a neural network, which supports the methodological foundation. The identified resonant frequencies (129 Hz, 172 Hz) serve as a benchmark to evaluate the proposed MFCC-based feature extraction. Critically, the study's limitations—small sample size, single variety, and controlled lab conditions—directly outline the gaps the present research addresses. This research aims to advance the approach by employing MFCCs for better noise resilience, using a larger local dataset, and testing in real field conditions to create a practical farm-level tool.\newline

\subsubsection{Evaluation Of Watermelon Ripeness Using Self Developed Ripening Detector}

The study investigates the use of a self-developed acoustic ripeness detector as an objective method for evaluating watermelon ripeness, offering an alternative to traditional subjective techniques such as manual tapping and visual inspection (Pamungkas & Bintoro, 2021). The primary purpose of the research is to determine whether acoustic impulse responses can reliably indicate ripeness levels. In the methodology, a mechanical tapping device was used to generate consistent impacts on the fruit, while the resulting sound signals were recorded and processed in MATLAB. Key acoustic features, including dominant frequency and magnitude, were extracted and analyzed across watermelons harvested at five different maturity stages.

The findings reveal that as watermelon maturity increased, both dominant frequency and magnitude decreased, showing strong correlation with fruit weight and soluble solids content, which are established indicators of ripeness. These results demonstrate that the detector can reliably differentiate ripeness levels based on acoustic characteristics. However, a notable limitation of the study is that it relies on a controlled experimental setup and requires mechanical tapping, which may limit practicality in large-scale or field applications. Additionally, the approach focuses on a limited set of acoustic features, potentially overlooking other informative signal characteristics.

This study is significant as it strongly validates the use of acoustic signals for non-destructive ripeness detection, providing a clear physiological basis for the proposed system. The inverse relationship between maturity and frequency/magnitude offers a crucial benchmark for acoustic modeling. The limitation of a controlled setup underscores the need for the present research to develop a portable solution for field use. Furthermore, the study highlights a key gap: its reliance on a narrow set of acoustic features could be complemented by integrating computer vision. By fusing acoustic data with visual analysis of external maturity indicators—such as ground-spot color and rind texture—a hybrid system could significantly improve classification robustness, addressing internal and external ripeness cues that a single-modality acoustic approach may miss.

\subsubsection{Non-Destructive Acoustic Screening of Pineapple Ripeness by Unsupervised Machine Learning and Wavelet Kernel Methods}

The study addresses the practical challenge of consistently screening pineapple ripeness on export production lines, where human workers are prone to fatigue-induced errors and subjective inconsistency after prolonged listening (Chen et al., 022). The purpose of the research is to develop an automated, non-destructive acoustic screening device capable of reliably classifying pineapples as raw or ripe directly on a conveyor system. The methodology involves injecting controlled excitation sounds into the fruit through a vibrator mediated by a custom acoustic coupler, then recording the penetrated sounds for analysis. A Taguchi experiment design was employed to systematically evaluate five acoustic coupler configurations across material, angle, and contact area parameters, ultimately identifying a screw-locked pinning bean board as the optimal coupler based on signal-to-noise ratio. 

The study explicitly demonstrates that conventional Fourier Transform analysis is insufficient for this application: both time-domain waveforms and frequency-domain spectral plots of raw and ripe pineapples appear visually indistinguishable, confirming that standard FFT cannot resolve the subtle acoustic differences caused by internal ripeness changes. To overcome this limitation, the study applies Morlet Wavelet Kernel decomposition, which provides simultaneous time and frequency resolution, offering high time resolution at high frequencies and high frequency resolution at low frequencies, allowing it to reveal structural differences that FFT cannot detect. The wavelet transform of ripe pineapples shows a clear spectral ridge at approximately 300 Hz, which is absent or muted in raw fruit, providing a discriminative feature for classification. These wavelet-derived features are processed through an unsupervised pipeline using cross-wavelet power spectrum coherence as the distance metric, followed by singular value decomposition for principal component extraction, and finally AdaBoost-based classification. 

From 124 original recordings totaling 3,475 sound frames after preprocessing, 2,780 unlabeled frames were used for unsupervised clustering and 695 labeled frames were reserved for testing. The system achieved 98.56\% classification accuracy with a 0.93 F1-score. A notable limitation is that approximately 70\% of initial recordings had to be discarded due to acoustic coupler quality issues, and the entire experiment was conducted under controlled, quiet laboratory conditions using a single pineapple variety, raising questions about robustness in noisy field environments. This study carries direct and substantial relevance to the proposed watermelon ripeness detector. Most critically, it provides empirical evidence that raw FFT fails to capture acoustic differences between ripeness stages in fruit tapping applications, because percussion sounds are non-periodic and transient rather than sinusoidal—a finding with direct implications for acoustic feature selection in any tapping-based ripeness system. 

This motivates the use of time-frequency representations such as Mel Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs) in the proposed system, which, like wavelet decomposition, simultaneously resolves temporal and spectral characteristics of short-duration signals. Additionally, the Taguchi-based acoustic coupler optimization provides a methodological precedent for the mechanical tapping and microphone placement design in the proposed device, reinforcing the importance of coupler geometry in achieving high signal quality. The study's unsupervised classification approach is also noteworthy: since ripeness is a continuous and gradually changing state rather than a binary condition, unsupervised or semi-supervised strategies may better reflect the underlying biology—a consideration that could inform future iterations of the proposed system when labeled training data from local watermelon varieties is limited.

\subsection{Machine Learning}

\subsubsection{Watermelon Ripeness Detector Using Near Infrared Spectroscopy}

The study presents the design and implementation of a low-cost, portable watermelon ripeness detection system based on near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS) and machine learning (Arboleda et al., 2020). Its main purpose is to provide a non-destructive and objective alternative to traditional ripeness assessment methods such as tapping or visual inspection, which are often inaccurate and subjective. The methodology involves collecting reflectance spectral data from the watermelon surface using a compact NIR sensor integrated with a Raspberry Pi Zero W. A total of 200 watermelon samples were analyzed and classified using a Support Vector Machine (SVM) to distinguish between ripe and unripe fruits.

The findings show that the proposed system achieved a classification accuracy of 92.5\%, demonstrating that reliable ripeness detection can be achieved even with a limited number of wavelength bands. This highlights the effectiveness of combining simple NIRS hardware with machine learning in real-world agricultural settings. However, a key limitation of the study is that it performs binary classification only and relies on a small dataset, which may limit generalizability across different watermelon varieties and growing conditions. Additionally, the use of manually selected wavelength bands and a single machine learning model may restrict scalability and robustness. 

Despite these limitations, the study is significant for the present research as it provides a validated proof-of-concept for a portable, sensor-based detection system, demonstrating the practical viability of deploying low-cost hardware in the field. The high accuracy achieved with a limited sensor also reinforces the potential for integrating other modalities, such as acoustic analysis, to move beyond binary classification and create a more comprehensive and nuanced ripeness assessment tool suitable for farm-level use.

\subsubsection{Supervised Binary Classification Methods for Strawberry Ripeness Discrimination from Bioimpedance Data}

The use of supervised machine learning for binary ripeness classification of strawberry fruit (Ibba et al., 2021) using sensor-derived features extracted from bioimpedance shows a reproducible ML pipeline, addressing the absence of a standardized methodology for this type of agricultural sensor data.. A total of 923 strawberry fruit were measured and labeled as ripe or unripe based on a CIELab color threshold, yielding 684 training and 239 test samples. The raw sensor data were reduced to 18 features through a two-stage selection process: a manual stage based on correlation analysis between features and ripeness labels, and an automated backward sequential feature selection combined with grid search hyperparameter tuning under ten-fold cross-validation. Six supervised binary classifiers were trained and compared: Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, Naive Bayes Classifier, K-Nearest Neighbors, Support Vector Machine, and Multi-Layer Perceptron. MLP models were optimized separately using a 60:20:20 training, validation, and internal test split to promote generalization, while the remaining five classifiers used 10,000-round bootstrap validation for stability assessment before final evaluation on the held-out test set.

This study is significant for the proposed watermelon ripeness detector as it provides a rigorously validated binary classification pipeline whose task structure, ripe versus unripe, directly mirrors the proposed system's classification objective. The complete pipeline spanning correlation-based manual feature selection, automated backward feature selection, bootstrap validation, and held-out test evaluation offers a methodological framework applicable to the proposed system's classifier development for both acoustic and tendril color features. The finding that MLP generalizes best to unseen data while SVM unexpectedly underperforms due to class overlap is a directly actionable result, cautioning against assuming SVM superiority in ripeness classification tasks where feature distributions may be non-linearly separable—a realistic concern when classifying tendril color under variable field conditions. Furthermore, the study's explicit design objective of deploying the developed models in a portable on-field device reinforces the feasibility of the proposed system's approach of combining optimized binary ML classification with accessible, field-ready hardware.

\subsection{Computer Vision}

\subsubsection{Application of Hyperspectral Imaging for Watermelon Seed Classification Using Deep Learning and Scoring Mechanism}

The study investigates the use of near-infrared hyperspectral imaging (NIR-HSI) integrated with deep learning techniques for the fast and non-destructive classification of watermelon seed varieties (Qi et al., 2024). Its primary purpose is to address the challenges of high dimensionality and noise in hyperspectral data while improving classification accuracy. Spectral data from 2,283 seeds across 10 varieties were captured within the 1053–1681 nm wavelength range, followed by region-of-interest extraction and spectral correction to ensure data consistency.

To address data complexity, the methodology integrates spectral preprocessing (Savitzky–Golay smoothing and Standard Normal Variate normalization) and feature selection using the Successive Projections Algorithm (SPA) to reduce redundant wavelength bands. Both traditional machine learning models (SVM, XGBoost) and deep learning models (LeNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet using 1D CNNs) were evaluated. The key finding of the study is that a weighted ensemble scoring mechanism, which fuses predictions from multiple deep learning models, significantly outperforms individual models, achieving a test accuracy of 87.97\%. The authors conclude that combining NIR-HSI, deep learning, and ensemble learning provides a robust and effective approach for accurate seed variety identification.
This study demonstrates advanced methodologies for processing high-dimensional agricultural sensor data. Its use of sophisticated preprocessing, feature selection, and ensemble modeling to handle complexity and improve accuracy provides a valuable conceptual framework for developing a classification system. 

However, the study's reliance on expensive, stationary hyperspectral imaging equipment presents a significant limitation, directly contrasting with the objective of creating a low-cost, portable field device. This distinction highlights the present research's focus on achieving practical deployability by utilizing accessible hardware and sensor fusion tailored for on-farm use.

\subsubsection{An Automated Pipeline for Supervised Classification of Petal Color from Citizen Science Photographs}

The study presents an automated computer vision pipeline for classifying plant color from field photographs using the hue-saturation-value (HSV) color space and k-means clustering, without the use of deep learning or neural networks  (Perez-Udell et al., 2023). Its purpose is to provide a flexible, computationally accessible method for classifying color variation in large photographic datasets, addressing the subjectivity and inconsistency inherent in manual scoring and the limitations of RGB-based analysis under variable lighting. 

The methodology consists of four sequential Python scripts: a downloader for image acquisition, a Color Cluster Visualizer that applies k-means clustering on a representative image subset to identify the HSV range of the target feature and determine the optimal number of clusters k, a Data Collector that performs the same clustering across all images and aggregates pixels within the defined HSV range into per-image summary statistics, and a Classifier that bins images into user-defined color categories based on those statistics. The pipeline is demonstrated on two plant species: Geranium maculatum, which displays continuous pink-to-purple petal variation classified into three bins using saturation thresholds, and Linanthus parryae, which displays a discrete binary color morph of white versus blue, classified by counting pixels falling within the blue HSV range against an empirically determined threshold. The binary classification case for L. parryae was validated by manual examination of a subset of images.

The findings demonstrate that the HSV-based pipeline produces accurate and repeatable color classifications without requiring controlled lighting or standardized camera conditions—a key advantage over RGB-based methods, in which illumination changes cause non-linear shifts in all three color channels simultaneously. For the binary classification of L. parryae, the pipeline achieves a precision of 91.5\% in identifying the blue morph, with all unclassified images defaulting correctly to the white morph. For the continuous G. maculatum case, the pipeline agrees with unanimous manual scoring 85.3\% of the time, and always agrees with at least one of three independent human scorers for contested images. The authors note that focusing classification solely on the pixel-level color range of the target feature, while intentionally ignoring shape and texture, eliminates angle and distance sensitivity and substantially reduces computational requirements, allowing the entire pipeline to run on consumer-grade hardware. 

A key limitation is that the method aggregates all pixels within the target color range regardless of spatial structure, meaning it cannot distinguish between separate co-occurring objects of the same color, and performs best when the target feature contrasts sufficiently with its background. This study is directly relevant to the computer vision component of the proposed watermelon ripeness detector, which classifies tendril color as either green or brown to indicate ripeness. The binary classification case demonstrated in this study—distinguishing two discrete plant color morphs using HSV thresholding and k-means segmentation—mirrors the proposed system's visual classification task structurally and methodologically. 

The finding that the HSV color space produces stable and consistent color classification under variable field lighting conditions, where RGB values would fluctuate non-linearly, directly supports the use of HSV as the color space of choice for tendril color extraction in the proposed system. The pipeline's pixel-counting approach for binary classification—counting pixels within a defined HSV range and applying a threshold—is a technically straightforward and computationally lightweight method well suited for embedded deployment in a portable device. 

Furthermore, the study's explicit discussion of the advantages of color-only segmentation over shape-and-texture-dependent methods reinforces the suitability of a color-focused traditional CV approach for the proposed system's tendril classification task, where the diagnostic information is entirely chromatic rather than structural.

\section{Synthesis}

The collective findings from these related studies establish a clear, converging pathway for the proposed research. Research by Wongkalasin et al. (2023) and Pamungkas /& Bintoro (2021) fundamentally validates acoustic analysis as a viable, non-destructive method for ripeness detection, demonstrating strong correlations between specific frequency features and internal fruit quality. However, these acoustic studies are constrained by controlled laboratory conditions, small sample sizes, and the limitation of single-sensor, single-variety testing, highlighting a critical gap for field application. In parallel, the work of Arboleda et al. (2020) provides a crucial proof-of-concept for a low-cost, portable system, but its scope is limited to binary classification. The study by Qi et al. (2024), while using different sensor technology, offers a sophisticated methodological blueprint for handling complex sensor data through advanced preprocessing and model fusion to boost accuracy. 

Synthesizing these insights, a significant opportunity is identified: to advance beyond these individual limitations by developing a portable, field-deployable tool. The proposed system directly addresses the identified gaps by integrating the validated principle of acoustic analysis—enhanced with more robust MFCC feature extraction for noise resilience—with computer vision for external assessment. This multi-modal, sensor-fusion approach, trained on a larger, locally-sourced dataset, aims to create a practical and comprehensive classification system that surpasses the scope, generalizability, and real-world applicability of the existing research, delivering a nuanced ripeness assessment tool specifically designed for farm-level use.
